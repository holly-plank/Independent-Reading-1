Making is Liberating

An exploration of the connections between making, creating, and technology integration and the implications for aspiring liberatory educators.

How can educators leverage the connections between making, creating, and technology integration to empower youth and provide the foundations for a liberatory education?

This project explores texts related to three key ideas that support the notion that "Making is Liberating".

1). Learning is constructing

2). Creating is connecting

3.) Connected learning is boundary-crossing.

These ideas provide a foundation for educators who aspire to provide youth with the environment and supports they need to construct their own liberatory education. The ultimate goal of this project is to provide educators with practical ways to empower youth through making, creating, and technology integration. The process of making and creating has the power to liberate education from the confines of an antiquated, oppressive schooling system. All children deserve a liberatory education.

Learning is Constructing

Learning does not happen in a vacuum. Children learn by making connections between past knowledge and experiences with new concepts. Children take on the role of maker when it comes to constructing their own knowledge, skills, and mindsets. Educators have the responsibility to nourish that ability and create the conditions necessary for learners to drive their education.

Aristotle

In Nicomachean Ethics, Aristotle sets the stage for the idea that we are making or inventing ourselves. This supports the idea that learning is how we construct aspects of our identities. While I agree with Aristotle's articulation of the approaches to knowledge and embracing change, his perspective is potentially limited in that he writes for and about "citizens" who were white, adult males at that time.

Aristotle claims that one of the goals for humans is to not stop at acquiring knowledge but to move to action. He demonstrates that individuals can, in fact, change. He outlines several approaches to knowledge including: episteme, techne, phronesis, praxis, and poiesis. These approaches move from knowing how to do something theoretically, knowing how to do something in practice, knowing when it is appropriate to do something, knowing how to apply something in a different context, and taking action.

Ackerman

Ackerman explores the difference between Piaget's concept of constructivism in education and Papert's constructionism. Papert believed that connectedness rather than separation from reality leads to knowledge and understanding. His theory of constructionism is at odds with educational philosophers that value abstract thinking over situated or connected thinking. When a child constructs their own knowledge through interactions with the world, they are able to make that knowledge tangible by expressing it in a creative way.

Papert

In Situating Constructionism, Seymour Papert and Idit Harel do not provide a straightforward definition of constructionism. They model their belief in the idea of constructionism by allowing the reader build on their initial model of thinking with additional evidence.

Unlike traditional epistemology that elevates more abstract forms of knowledge, Papert and Harel bring forth feminist scholarship revealing that women thrive with "more personal, less-detached knowledge." In Papert's various works, he reimagines the role of teacher as someone who creates the conditions for discovery where the student can drive their own learning.

Making is Connecting

Learning happens through the process of constructing, which leads to the construction of aspects of individual identity over time. Through the process of making and creating, learners connect with the world. In these texts, the authors focus on creative identity, maker identity, and computer science identity. Making, creating, and technology integration do not happen in isolation. They are inherently connected to society and the relationship of the individual to the collective.

Gauntlett

David Gauntlett describes the evolution of an individual's creative identity and its relationship to society and multiple others. Creative identity is not something that is a given or straight-forward. In fact, Gauntlett demonstrates that it may be denied, problematic, or a source of anxiety for individuals.

He also argues that an individual's identity as a creative person is constructed over time through "struggles and acts of resistance." This idea of resistance through making and creating sets the stage for liberation.

Cavarero

Adriana Cavarero deconstructs the writings of Plato through feminist analysis in her book, In Spite of Plato: A Feminist Rewriting of Ancient Philosophy. Caverero, like Papert, is a materialist. She illustrates that making is connecting in her rewriting of the stories of Penelope, the Maidservant from Thrace, Demeter, and Diotima. She contrasts women and men with birth and death. Birth is the ultimate act of making or creating. Cavarero demonstrates the power of making in Penelope's act of liberating herself weaving and unraveling her work.

In the process of liberation, it is sometimes important to deconstruct before constructing.

Connected Learning is Boundary-Crossing

Learning is a set of boundary-crossings seen primarily in Connected Learning Theory. The process of making crosses boundaries. Boundary-crossing is one step toward a liberatory education.

Research on Connected Learning

Learning is a series of boundary-crossings in and across social spaces according to Kumpulainen and Sefton-Green. This can include social spaces like home, school, work, and social relationships. However, this also includes educational contexts like in and out of school learning, different content areas, and epistemic practices.

Bilandzic highlights the need for connection in learning along lines of difference. Knowledge is more powerful when it is co-created. Ito, et.al. highlight the need for education to be a pathway to opportunity. Connected learning can address equity gaps in education. The ability for connected learning to ensure that learning is "socially embedded, interest-driven, and oriented toward educational, economic, or political opportunity" can move beyond an equitable education to a liberatory education. Connected learning combines learning and design principles that build on the work of Papert and Dewey.

Gee

James Paul Gee connects to Aristotle's stages of knowledge with recommendations for smart human action. He claims that people need to build on prior experience, engage with a mentor, set clear goals, be invested emotionally, and recognize an opportunity to act.

"We are reciprocal and mutual tools for each other, networked together as a more powerful thinking unit." Gee imagines that human minds are wired for connection and that connection makes us more powerful.

Conclusion: Making is Liberating

The entire process of exploring texts related to making, creating, and technology integration has been constructionist in nature. As I have encountered new writers, approaches to writing, and topics related to making, creating, and technology integration; I have made connections based on my past experience as a learner and educator. There have been times where I have needed to deconstruct my previous understandings before reconstructing something new. I have come to the conclusion that the process of making and creating has the power to liberate. While I have pushed for an equitable education for all students in my career as an educator, liberation breaks down the barriers that make equitable supports necessary in the first place.

Especially in the time of the COVID-19 pandemic, technology is a tool that can constrain or liberate education. While the pandemic has undoubtedly caused unimaginable harm and loss, it can also be an opportunity to rethink how we can liberate education from the confines of traditional schooling and epistemology that are often racist and sexist. It is time to move beyond seeking equality or even equity in education. All children deserve a liberatory education, and making is liberating.

Implications for Teacher Education

Inevitably, preservice and inservice teachers may not have experienced the kind of schooling and education that our children need. While some people get into teaching because they want to know and do better, many aspiring teachers loved school. It worked for them. Papert implores us to acknowledge that the traditional knowledge-set in K-12 schoos may be sufficient for some students, but it is "neither necessary or optimal." What does it take to shift the mindsets of aspiring teachers? Keengwe, Onchwari, and Wachira argue that teachers must experience technology integration themselves as students. The same is true for making and creating. As humans, we are wired to resist change. The discomfort that educators might experience in reimagining education in this way can lead to sustainable change in their practice. That change can be path changing for their students.
